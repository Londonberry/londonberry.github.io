Every founder I talk to asks some version of the same question. "We're not big enough for a real CTO yet. But things keep breaking and I don't know what to do about it. What's between zero and a $300K hire?"

The answer is usually a fractional CTO. But not always. Here's the framework.

## What a fractional CTO actually is

A fractional CTO is a senior engineering leader you retain part-time, usually 8 to 20 hours a week, on a monthly retainer. They own the technical seat the way a full-time CTO would: architecture, hiring, vendor selection, code review, roadmap, investor diligence support, incident response. They just don't do it 40 hours a week, and they're not on your cap table.

It's a different thing from a contractor (who executes against a spec you write) and a different thing from an advisor (who gives you opinions on a monthly call but doesn't own anything). A fractional CTO is accountable for the technical outcome of your business. That accountability is the product.

## The five-question test

You don't need a fractional CTO because someone told you to. You need one when specific things become true. Five questions:

**1. Do you have a working product but no senior engineer who owns it?**

A non-technical founder shipped something with Lovable or hired a contract dev for the MVP. The code exists. Nobody's accountable for it. Things break and nobody knows whose fault it is. This is the most common trigger and the easiest case to solve.

**2. Are you about to hire engineers and you've never managed engineers before?**

You're going to make hiring mistakes worth $100K to $500K each. A fractional CTO can run the interview loop, level candidates, and prevent the worst ones. Most engagements pay for themselves on the first hire avoided.

**3. Are you raising and your tech story is shaky?**

Investors do technical diligence. You need someone who can talk to them in their language about your stack, your architecture, your security posture, and your roadmap. A polished tech story (told by someone with the experience to back it up) closes rounds.

**4. Are you a solo technical founder making calls you're too in-the-weeds to second-guess?**

You can ship code. That's not the problem. The problem is you have nobody to tell you when you're about to commit to a database choice you'll regret in 18 months, or when your AI tooling setup is quietly accumulating security debt, or when the architecture you're proud of has a fatal flaw a senior engineer would spot in 30 seconds.

**5. Is your team velocity dropping and you can't tell why?**

Two to five engineers, shipping less and less. Could be tech debt. Could be a hiring mistake. Could be that the founder is the bottleneck. A fractional CTO can diagnose in two weeks what a founder might wrestle with for two quarters.

If you said yes to two or more, you probably need one. If you said yes to four or five, you needed one six months ago.

## What a fractional CTO is not

A fractional CTO is not a code monkey. They will write code occasionally (often during a rescue, or as part of an architecture spike) but they are not your contractor. If you mostly need someone to ship features, hire a contractor or a contract dev team. Cheaper, faster, more honest.

A fractional CTO is not a therapist. They will tell you when your technical choices are bad. That is the value. Founders who want validation should hire a different person.

A fractional CTO is not infinitely scalable. The retainer model works because the engagement is bounded. If your needs grow to 30+ hours a week consistently for more than a quarter, you need a full-time hire, and a good fractional CTO will tell you that and help you hire them.

## What it costs

Honest numbers, since most pages dance around this:

Fractional CTO retainers in the US market in 2026 range from about $8K/month at the low end (10 hours/week, generalist) to about $25K/month at the high end (20+ hours/week, specialized, senior). The median for an experienced operator is around $12K to $18K a month.

That sounds like a lot until you compare it to:

- A full-time CTO ($250K to $400K base, plus equity, plus benefits, plus the cost of being wrong about the hire).
- A bad architectural decision discovered 18 months in ($100K to $1M to unwind).
- A bad engineering hire ($100K to $500K).
- A botched investor diligence call (the round you didn't close).

For a pre-seed or seed-stage company burning $30K to $80K a month in payroll already, a fractional CTO is one of the highest-leverage line items you can add.

## How to evaluate one

Three questions to ask any fractional CTO before you hire them:

**"Tell me about a project where you were wrong."** People who can't answer this are dangerous. Senior judgment comes from being wrong publicly and learning from it. If they only have wins, they don't have judgment, they have luck.

**"What would you do in the first 30 days here?"** A good answer is specific: "I'd read all the code, talk to every engineer, audit your auth and data model, and produce a written risk report." A bad answer is generic: "I'd get to know the team and assess the situation."

**"When would you fire yourself?"** A good answer names the conditions under which you should bring in a full-time CTO instead. A bad answer dodges. Fractional CTOs who are honest about the limits of fractional work are the ones worth keeping.

## The bigger picture

The fractional CTO role exists because the gap between "I have an idea" and "I have a company that needs a full-time CTO" got much wider in the last three years. AI tools let founders ship working software faster than ever. They did not let founders make better technical decisions. The judgment gap is where companies get killed.

A fractional CTO fills that gap for the 12 to 24 months when you need senior judgment but can't justify (or attract) a senior hire. Used well, it's one of the most cost-effective moves a founder can make. Used poorly, it's an expensive advisor.

Pick carefully. Ask the hard questions. And don't wait until something has already broken to make the call.
