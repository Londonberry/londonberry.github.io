The [2026 FIFA World Cup](https://www.fifa.com/en/tournaments/mens/worldcup/canadamexicousa2026) is the biggest in history by every measure. 48 teams instead of 32. 104 matches instead of 64. Three host countries across two borders. It kicked off June 11 and runs to the final at MetLife Stadium on July 19.

It is also the most heavily instrumented sporting event ever staged. Under the noise of the largest tournament ever, there's a layer of AI and sensor tech quietly making calls that used to take a referee and three replays to sort out.

## Semi-Automated Offside, Now Routine

Offside used to be the slowest, most argued-about call in the sport. A linesman with a flag, a frozen frame, and a lot of opinions.

[Semi-automated offside technology](https://www.fifa.com/en/technology/football-technology) changed that. Cameras mounted around the stadium track up to 29 data points on every player, 50 times per second. The system knows where every limb is at the exact moment the ball is played. When an attacker is offside, it builds the decision automatically and pushes a 3D animation to the broadcast within seconds.

What used to be a guess is now a measurement. The referee still makes the final call, but the machine does the part humans were bad at: seeing a 4-inch margin across 60 yards in real time.

## A Match Ball That Reports Its Own Touches

Inside the official ball sits a sensor sending position data 500 times per second. Pair that with the camera tracking and the system knows the exact instant the ball is kicked. That precision is what makes the offside calls trustworthy, and it kills the old argument about which frame to freeze.

It also catches things the eye misses entirely. Subtle touches, deflections, the difference between a handball and a ball off the shoulder. The data exists now whether or not anyone trusts it yet.

## Every Player, Every Touch, Logged

Beyond officiating, every match produces a flood of tracking data. Distance covered, sprint speed, passing lanes, defensive shape. Broadcasters turn it into graphics. Coaches turn it into game plans. Betting markets turn it into prices.

The interesting shift is that this used to be a competitive edge a handful of clubs paid for. Now it's table stakes, generated automatically for every team in the tournament. The advantage moved from *having* the data to *doing something useful with it faster than everyone else.*

## The Lesson for Anyone Building Products

The World Cup tech story is not really about soccer. It's about a pattern we see in every industry that gets instrumented.

First, you measure the thing nobody could measure before. Then the measurement gets cheap and universal. Then the edge stops being access to data and starts being judgment: what you ask of it, how fast you act, and whether you trust it enough to change a decision.

That last part is the hard one. FIFA spent years getting referees and fans comfortable with a machine drawing the offside line. The technology was ready well before the trust was. Same story in most companies sitting on data they don't act on.

The teams that win the next decade, in sport and in business, are not the ones with the most sensors. They're the ones who built the judgment to use what the sensors already tell them.
