Most "AI in sports" articles are a list of things that might happen. This is a list of things already running, in real games, right now. We work in sports tech, so we see which of these actually ship and which stay in the demo. Here are ten that have crossed over, ranked roughly by how mature and widespread they are.

## 1. Officiating and Line Calls

The most visible use, and the most mature. Semi-automated offside at the World Cup, electronic line calling that replaced human judges across pro tennis, automated ball-strike systems climbing through pro baseball. AI doesn't get tired, doesn't have a bad angle, and measures margins humans were never able to see. This is the use case where the technology was ready years before fans were comfortable with it.

## 2. Injury Prevention and Player Load

Wearables and computer vision track how much load an athlete is absorbing: sprint counts, deceleration, asymmetry in movement. Models flag the patterns that precede soft-tissue injuries so staff can rest a player before the hamstring goes, not after. The teams that do this well lose fewer player-games to injury, which is worth more than almost any free-agent signing.

## 3. Broadcast and Highlight Generation

AI now cuts highlight packages automatically, tracks the ball for on-screen graphics, and generates real-time commentary and win-probability overlays. What used to need a production truck and an editor is now a stream you toggle on. This is where AI is quietly making the fan experience better without anyone needing to know it's there.

## 4. Scouting and Player Evaluation

Every pitch, swing, stride, and shot is now tracked and profiled. Scouting a prospect used to be a career's worth of accumulated eye. Now it's a query against a database, with the human eye spent on the things data still misses: makeup, work ethic, how a kid responds to failure. The edge moved from having the numbers to knowing which ones matter.

## 5. Game Strategy and In-Game Decisions

Matchup models, defensive positioning, when to go for it on fourth down. Coaches use AI to pressure-test decisions that used to run on gut alone. The good ones treat it as a second opinion, not an autopilot. The data tells you the odds. A human still has to read the room.

## 6. Fan Personalization and Content

Streaming services and team apps use AI to recommend what to watch, build personalized highlight feeds, and surface the moment a casual fan actually cares about. The same recommendation tech that runs retail now runs the relationship between a league and its audience.

## 7. Ticketing and Dynamic Pricing

AI prices tickets the way airlines price seats: demand, opponent, weather, team form, all feeding a price that moves in real time. Done well it fills seats and grows revenue. Done badly it alienates the season-ticket base. The model is the easy part. The judgment about when to override it is the hard part.

## 8. Computer Vision for Tracking

Underneath half this list is one technology: cameras plus models that turn raw video into structured data about where every player and the ball are, frame by frame. It is the foundation that officiating, scouting, broadcast graphics, and strategy all sit on top of. When you hear "tracking data," this is what generated it.

## 9. Sports Betting and Risk Modeling

Whatever you think of it, betting is one of the most aggressive adopters of real-time AI in sports. Live odds reprice in milliseconds off the same tracking data the teams use. The market is, in effect, a giant prediction model running on the game as it happens.

## 10. Athlete Training and Biomechanics

Markerless motion capture and AI analysis let a pitcher or a sprinter see exactly how their mechanics break down under fatigue, then drill the fix. What used to require a lab and a day of setup now runs off a phone camera and a model. Player development is becoming a software problem.

## What This List Actually Tells You

Step back from the specifics and a pattern shows up. None of these are AI replacing the human. Every one of them is AI removing friction between a person and a decision they were already trying to make. The referee still rules. The coach still calls it. The scout still judges character. AI just hands them a measurement they couldn't get before.

That's the lesson worth taking into your own product, whether or not you're anywhere near sports. The AI that ships and sticks is rarely the kind that replaces a person. It's the kind that makes a person faster, sharper, and more confident in a call they already had to make.

If you're trying to figure out where AI actually fits in what you're building, that's the question we help teams answer. Not "what can AI do," but "which decision in your business is slow, expensive, or guesswork, and could a measurement fix it." Start there and most of the hype falls away.
