You've decided you need senior technical help. Good. Now the next question, which is where most founders get lost: which kind?

Three roles get pitched constantly. The names sound similar. The pricing is wildly different. The outcomes are not interchangeable. Let's untangle them.

## The three roles

**Fractional CTO.** A senior engineering leader on retainer, 8 to 20 hours a week, accountable for the technical seat. They own architecture, hiring, code review, vendor selection, and incident response. They are part of your operating team.

**Tech Lead (contractor or fractional).** A senior engineer who can run a small team or own a workstream, 20 to 40 hours a week. They write code, mentor juniors, ship features. They are part of your delivery team.

**CTO Advisor.** A senior CTO (often at another company) you meet with monthly. They give opinions, make introductions, and pressure-test decisions. They are not part of any team. They are a sounding board.

The shorthand: an advisor talks, a tech lead builds, a fractional CTO owns.

## How to tell which one you need

Five questions:

**Do you need someone to make decisions or someone to react to them?**

Advisor: react. Tech lead: react to a manager. Fractional CTO: make them. If you're hoping someone will tell you the right architecture instead of validate your choice, you don't want an advisor.

**Do you have engineers who need a manager?**

If yes, you need a fractional CTO (to set direction and hire well) or a tech lead (to manage the day-to-day). If no engineers yet, an advisor + a contract dev team can hold you over.

**Are you raising in the next 6 months?**

If yes, you need a fractional CTO. Investors do technical diligence. An advisor is not a substitute for someone on your team who can answer the architecture question on the call.

**Is your problem strategic or tactical?**

Strategic (what do we build, what stack, who do we hire, when do we rewrite): fractional CTO or advisor. Tactical (ship this feature, fix this bug, run this migration): tech lead.

**What's your monthly burn for this line item?**

Advisor: $500 to $3000/mo. Tech lead: $15K to $30K/mo for a senior contractor. Fractional CTO: $8K to $25K/mo. Set expectations before you start interviewing or you'll waste everyone's time.

## What each one is actually worth

**Advisors are worth it when:** you have a specific recurring decision in your future (raising a round, big architecture shift, leadership change) and want someone with scars from the same fight to think it through with you monthly. They're not worth it as "general guidance" most months. The conversations get vague.

**Tech leads are worth it when:** you have engineers, you don't have a manager, and the shipping problem is real. They will not solve your hiring problem. They will not own your roadmap. They will ship.

**Fractional CTOs are worth it when:** the technical seat is empty and the cost of leaving it empty is high. Pre-seed and seed founders, especially non-technical ones, almost always benefit. Series A founders sometimes benefit (it can be a bridge while you hire full-time). Series B and beyond, you usually need a full-time CTO or a head of engineering, not a fractional anything.

## The combinations that actually work

The most effective stack we see at pre-seed/seed:

**Fractional CTO + contract engineers.** The fractional CTO sets direction and manages. The contractors ship. You pay a quarter of what a full-time team would cost and you get senior judgment plus delivery capacity.

**Advisor + fractional CTO.** Less common, but powerful when the advisor has been a CEO themselves and the fractional CTO is operationally focused. The advisor coaches the founder; the fractional CTO runs the engineering org.

**Tech lead + advisor.** Works at very early seed when there's already one engineer (often the founder) and you need them to have someone shipping alongside, plus a more senior voice for the big calls.

What rarely works: **tech lead + fractional CTO** at the same time, paying full retainer for both. Either the tech lead doesn't have enough autonomy or the fractional CTO doesn't have enough to own. Usually one role.

## The mistakes founders make

**Hiring an advisor when they need a CTO.** Advisors give opinions. Opinions are cheap. You needed someone to actually run a hiring loop, evaluate two architectural options for you, and call the shot. The advisor said "interesting tradeoffs!" and your runway burned.

**Hiring a tech lead when they need a fractional CTO.** Tech leads ship. But they don't own the things that are killing you: hiring, vendor selection, the AI guardrails, the investor diligence story. You got more features and the same strategic gap.

**Hiring a fractional CTO when they actually need a full-time CTO.** If you genuinely need 30+ hours a week, week after week, you don't have a fractional engagement, you have a part-time CTO doing full-time work badly. Pay for full-time or scope down what you need.

**Hiring nobody for too long.** The cheapest of all options up front. The most expensive over six months. Every architectural decision you punt becomes more expensive to revisit. Every hire you make without a senior interviewer is a bet with a 30% bust rate.

## The bigger picture

The advisor / tech lead / fractional CTO distinction matters because the wrong choice doesn't just waste money. It papers over the real problem for six months and lets you discover it after you've already raised, hired, or shipped. The right choice gets you the right kind of accountability for the stage you're in.

Pick by the question you can't answer alone. Not by the budget. Not by the title. By the question.
